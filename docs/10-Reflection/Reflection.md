## Review of Module's Success

My module met every requirement, and functions completely. I achieved every one of my goals and even some extra stuff, like saving the captured images to an SD card. The only part of my board that did not work, was one of my three LEDs, but as only one was actually required, there's no issue.

## Microcontroller/Module Startup Tip

Based on my experience creating my own board, the following is a list of tips for getting your module working:
- **Have a power indicator LED:** A power indicator LED is really nice, it immediately lets you know that your voltage regulator is working, and that your board is receiving power. It's a small addition but it's pretty helpful
- **Have plenty of jumpers:** If you need to rewire your board, having spare jumpers is very convenient. Also helps for testing and debugging.
- **Don't have tiny components:** Even though the idea of having a small compact board is nice, make sure your components are big enough to solder to save a lot of headaches
- **Be lucky:** Some team members in my group got several components broken upon arrival. So make sure you're lucky so that can't happen.
- **Buy extra components:** I did not buy enough spare components, and although it ended up fine, it's a good idea to have a backup plan.

## Lessons Learned

The top 10 most important things I've learned from this project are the following:
1. **Double check everything:** If you make one mistake in this project, it could be minimal, or it could make your board completely un-usable. So make absolutely sure everything is in order before you sign off on it.
2. **Have backup plans:** If things break there should ideally be some sort of failsafe or backup plan.
3. **Label stuff:** Even if I think I will remember stuff, I won't so I should label what things do, and what they are.
4. **Keep things organized:** This goes along with the tip above. To make sure you know what's going on you should have things organized otherwise you might forget.
5. **Communicate with your team:** Especially if you have a larger team, ideas and instructions may not get passed along very well, so one should make sure to keep asking questions and trying to make sure everybody is on the same page.
6. **Delagate:** In a team project somebody has to figure out who's doing what, or nothing's gonna get done. Otherwise you end up scrambling to finish an assignment at the last moment, because nobody wanted to be the guy to get things started.
7. **Join smaller teams:** My team was great, and everybody did well, but the group was way too big. Smaller groups are better since it's easier to communicate, plan, and all be available at the same time.
8. **Make sure you double check components before soldering:** Make sure your components are correct, not backwards, and properly aligned. Desoldering components is a pain, especially if there's other components nearby. Plus soldering and desoldering repeatedly has a chance to ruin your board. So make sure everything is correct before you commit.
9. **Get plenty of backup components:** Even if you don't end up needing them, other teammates might. Several people in my group exchanged components, so it's a great idea to have plenty of extras, just in case.
10. **Make your goals achievable:** In this project there was a lot more freedom to choose the function of our boards, and I decided to go with a camera which almost became a real problem. In retrospect going with something a bit more simple, with optional fancier stretch goals would have been a better idea.

## Recommendations for Future Students
Below are my top 5 recommendations for future students:
1. **Join a good team:** My team was great, but if they weren't it would have been very bad. Getting in a good group is mostly luck, but it can be very impactful to your success.
2. **Be a good team member:** Do your job, and help other team members. Don't be the person in the group project that sits around and does nothing, nobody wants to be that guy.
3. **Triple check your PCB:** If your PCB is incorrect there's sometimes very little you can do to fix it. Make sure you are **certain** there are no major issues with the board before you submit it for fabrication.
4. **Add a bunch of headers:** If, despite checking your PCB, it still has problems, make sure you have plenty of headers. They make debugging, testing, and fixing small issues significantly easier. I had headers on almost every pin coming from my microcontroller, and I regret nothing, you should too.
5. **Try to finish ahead of time:** Since the class wraps up near finals week, having your board completed before then means you can shift your focus to your other classes. Plus it's much nicer than trying to scramble and get things working at the last minute.
